The Human Brain

The human brain weighs about 1.4 kilograms — roughly the size of two fists held together. It looks like a wrinkled grey mass, soft to the touch, and it floats in a clear protective fluid inside your skull. It consumes about 20% of all the energy your body uses, despite being only 2% of your body weight. Inside this unremarkable-looking organ sits everything you have ever thought, felt, remembered, imagined, or feared. Every word you know, every person you love, every skill you have learned — all of it is somehow encoded in three pounds of biological matter.

The brain is made of about 86 billion neurons — specialised cells that communicate by sending electrical and chemical signals to each other. Each neuron connects to thousands of others through junctions called synapses. When you learn something new, your brain physically changes — synaptic connections strengthen or weaken, new connections form, old ones fade away. This ability to change is called neuroplasticity, and it continues throughout your entire life, though it is most rapid in childhood. The brain is not a fixed machine. It is alive and constantly remodelling itself based on experience.

Your brain is divided into regions that specialise in different tasks, though they constantly work together. The prefrontal cortex, just behind your forehead, handles planning, decision-making, and self-control. It is the last region of the brain to fully develop — not completing its maturation until your mid-twenties. The amygdala, two almond-shaped clusters deep inside the brain, processes emotions and fear responses. The hippocampus is critical for forming new memories. The cerebellum, at the back, coordinates movement and balance. None of these regions work in isolation — even a simple action like catching a ball involves dozens of regions working in parallel.

Memory is not stored like files on a hard drive. Memories are patterns of connections spread across many brain regions simultaneously. Each time you recall a memory, you are partly reconstructing it — pulling together pieces and reassembling them, sometimes slightly differently each time. This is why eyewitness testimony can be unreliable, and why memories can feel vivid even when they are inaccurate. Sleep plays a crucial role in memory consolidation. While you sleep, your brain replays the experiences of the day, strengthening important neural patterns and discarding noise. Learning without adequate sleep is significantly less effective, no matter how hard you study.

Emotions are not separate from rational thinking — they are deeply integrated with it. The emotional and reasoning centres of the brain are constantly in communication. Damage to emotional-processing regions can actually impair decision-making, even when logic and language remain intact. Neuroscientist Antonio Damasio studied patients with damage to emotional brain regions and found they struggled to make good decisions despite having intact reasoning abilities. This suggests that emotions provide crucial information to guide choices, not just colour them. Intuition — that feeling that something is right or wrong before you can explain why — may be your emotional brain communicating faster than your verbal mind can articulate.

The brain generates consciousness — the felt sense of being you, of experiencing the world from the inside — but how it does this remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in science. We can map which regions activate during different mental states. We can predict some behaviour from brain scans. But we cannot yet explain why there is something it feels like to be you, why there is a subjective experience at all rather than just information processing in the dark. This is sometimes called the hard problem of consciousness. Some philosophers and scientists argue it is the hardest question humanity has ever faced.

Your brain processes an enormous amount of information but is highly selective about what reaches your conscious awareness. You are surrounded by sensory input at every moment — the feel of your clothes, background noise, the peripheral edges of your vision — yet most of it never reaches consciousness. The brain continuously filters, predicts, and prioritises, constructing a version of reality rather than simply recording it. When you read a sentence with a wrod misplelled, you often do not notice — your brain has already predicted what the word should be and filled in the gap. Perception is as much about prediction as it is about input.

The brain can learn at any age, but the nature of learning changes over time. Children have intensely plastic brains — they absorb language, social norms, and motor skills with astonishing efficiency. This is because their synaptic connections are being pruned and refined rapidly, shaped by every experience. In adulthood, learning requires more deliberate effort, but the brain remains adaptable. Musicians who practice intensively show measurable changes in the regions controlling finger movement. Taxi drivers who navigate complex cities develop larger spatial memory regions. The brain responds to what you repeatedly do and think — making your habits, both mental and physical, among the most powerful forces shaping who you become.
